A compact workshop could use zones, not clutter.

The point of the layout is to reduce friction. A non-technical person should know where to stand, what to touch, what not to touch, and who to ask.

Tool wall

Hand tools visible, labelled and returned to outlines so missing items are obvious.

Rolling repair bench

A movable bench with power, light, clamps, basic fasteners and repair trays.

Small machine bay

Drill press, sander, small saw and dust control behind clear induction rules.

Parts library

Sorted screws, fittings, cables, wheels, hinges, brackets and reusable components.

Material rack

Reclaimed timber, sheet offcuts, tubes, rods, safe plastics and test samples stored by size.

Clean media desk

A small laptop/camera area for notices, how-to cards, photos, QR labels and experiment logs.

What tools earn their shelf?

A public tool list should be shaped by common use, safety, repair value and storage pressure, not by excitement alone.

First shelf

Measuring, marking, screwdrivers, pliers, spanners, socket sets, clamps, files, hand saws, hot glue, cable tools and basic PPE.

Repair shelf

Multimeter, soldering station, small driver bits, bike tools, pump parts, sewing repair kit, adhesive tests and cleaning supplies.

Fabrication shelf

Drill press, jigsaw, scroll saw, sander, rotary tool, small CNC or laser only after ventilation, supervision and demand are clear.

Material shelf

Sieves, scales, moulds, hand lenses, sample jars, small kiln access by partnership, and safe demonstration kits for sand and glass literacy.

Shared tool shelf

Tool sharing can be careful, not casual.

A shared tool library only works when trust is designed in. The Markdown forms can capture who stewards a tool, what training is needed, how check-out works, what consumables cost, and what should never be publicly listed.

Safety can be welcoming.

A public maker-space does not need scary gatekeeping. It needs clear colours, visible inductions, calm supervision, tool categories, easy reporting, tidy storage, and a culture where stopping is normal.

How could the room feel open and careful at the same time?

Right-to-repair is strongest when people can learn without pretending every repair is simple. Some objects are perfect teaching cases. Some need a specialist. Some should be recycled. A good lab makes those pathways visible.
